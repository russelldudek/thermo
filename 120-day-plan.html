Days 1-60

Read the system. Choose the proof.

Days 1-30 · Read

  • Map strategy, platforms, standards, governance, active demand, stakeholders, and recurring friction.
  • Interview business, scientific, data, cloud, security, privacy, compliance, product, automation, and operations leaders.
  • Baseline review cycle time, reuse, reliability, cost visibility, adoption, and exception age.

Days 31-60 · Choose

  • Select one bounded, decision-critical use case with accountable business and technical owners.
  • Create the architecture contract and evidence threshold.
  • Resolve build, buy, reuse, or partner choices and run a pre-mortem.

Day-60 exit criteria

  • Architecture landscape and friction map are agreed enough to act.
  • One bounded proof has accountable owners.
  • Outcome, authority, controls, integration, operations, and evidence are explicit.
  • Known risks are visible before build momentum.

Days 61-90

Run one reference pattern.

Execution guidance

  • Translate the architecture contract into components, interfaces, policies, tests, and operating responsibilities.
  • Make RAG, agent, data, API, microservice, and RPA choices explicit and reversible where possible.
  • Keep human decision rights and exception handling visible.
  • Instrument traces, logs, metrics, evaluation, access evidence, lineage, cost, latency, failure, adoption, and support signals.
  • Define SLOs, incident ownership, escalation, rollback, and change controls.

Review gates

Value
Does the workflow outcome earn the operating burden?
Authority
Are data and human decision rights explicit?
Trust
Do evaluation and controls match consequence?
Operations
Are support, incident, rollback, and cost ownership ready?

Days 91-120

Compound the learning.

System changes

  • Convert proof into reference patterns, templates, control requirements, evaluation harnesses, integration contracts, IaC guidance, cost models, and runbooks.
  • Establish a cadence to promote, revise, hold, retire, or exempt patterns.
  • Clarify decision rights across architecture, platforms, squads, governance, data, and business leaders.
  • Treat squad friction as architecture-product backlog.

Balanced scorecard

Day-120 exit criteria

  • One bounded pattern has a clear evidence-based disposition.
  • Friction and decision rights are more legible than on day one.
  • Reusable assets exist where squads actually work.
  • Leadership can see whether architecture improves speed, trust, reliability, cost, and adoption.
