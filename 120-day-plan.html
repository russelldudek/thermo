Operating premise and days 1-30

Earn the right to standardize.

The first objective is not to introduce a new architecture regime. It is to understand where Thermo Fisher's existing standards already accelerate scientific and business teams, where legitimate variation must remain, and where recurring friction signals a missing enterprise capability.

What should be materially different by day 120

One shared architecture map
Platforms, decision rights, active demand, risk boundaries, review friction, and ownership are legible enough to guide action.
One bounded proof
A decision-critical AI use case has explicit outcome, authority, control, integration, operating, adoption, and evidence contracts.
One reusable pattern
The proof produces a documented disposition and assets that another squad can adopt, adapt, or knowingly decline.
One operating cadence
Architecture decisions, exceptions, cost, reliability, adoption, and learning are reviewed through a practical PPI-compatible mechanism.

Days 1-30 · Read the system before prescribing

Stakeholder topology

  • Business and scientific sponsors: outcomes, consequence, timing, and adoption burden.
  • Enterprise, solution, data, cloud, integration, and security architects: current principles, paved roads, exceptions, and technical debt.
  • Privacy, compliance, legal, quality, and risk leaders: evidence thresholds and non-negotiable controls.
  • Product, automation, squad, operations, support, and FinOps leaders: delivery friction, run burden, economics, and ownership gaps.

Evidence baseline

  • Inventory active AI demand by workflow, decision, business owner, platform, risk class, and lifecycle state.
  • Trace a representative request from intake through architecture review, delivery, release, operation, and support.
  • Baseline review latency, recurring exceptions, duplicated components, reliability events, cost visibility, retained use, and unresolved ownership.
  • Separate documented facts from hypotheses requiring discovery; avoid treating tool inventory as architecture understanding.

Days 31-60

Choose a proof that can teach the enterprise.

The first proof should be important enough to expose real architecture tradeoffs, bounded enough to finish responsibly, and reusable enough to leave more than a one-off success behind.

Proof-selection criteria

LensQualification questionDisqualifying signal
Outcome and consequenceIs there a named workflow, decision, owner, baseline, and observable result?General enthusiasm without accountable outcome ownership
Data and authorityAre authoritative sources, entitlements, human decision rights, abstention, and escalation identifiable?Unclear data authority or implied autonomous action without an owner
Enterprise reuseWill the work exercise a pattern likely to recur across products, functions, or scientific workflows?A bespoke edge case with little transferable learning
Integration and operabilityCan interfaces, observability, incidents, rollback, cost, and lifecycle ownership be made explicit?No credible production or support owner
Adoption burdenCan the affected people test, challenge, learn, and retain the changed workflow?Success depends on communications alone rather than changed work

The architecture contract

1. Outcome boundary
Workflow, decision, customer or scientific consequence, owner, baseline, and measurement method.
2. Authority boundary
Authoritative context, model inference, human judgment, permitted actions, abstention, and escalation.
3. Control boundary
Privacy, security, compliance, quality, lineage, retention, evaluation, and audit evidence matched to consequence.
4. Integration boundary
APIs, events, microservices, data products, ERP, CRM, instruments, RPA, and external dependencies.
5. Operating boundary
SLOs, traces, logs, metrics, drift, incidents, rollback, capacity, unit cost, and support ownership.
6. Reuse boundary
Pattern owner, versioning, documentation, exception logic, adoption support, and promotion evidence.

Pre-mortem before build momentum

Failure modes to surface

  • Grounding appears strong in a demo but fails under real scientific language or freshness requirements.
  • Security and privacy controls arrive after the integration shape is already expensive to change.
  • Model quality is measured while workflow quality, human authority, and operational burden remain invisible.
  • Cost, latency, support, and adoption make the solution uneconomic despite technical success.

Decisions to record

  • Build, buy, reuse, partner, or stop—and the evidence behind the choice.
  • What is standardized, what remains local, and who can approve an exception.
  • Which risks are mitigated, accepted, transferred, or still unresolved.
  • What evidence is required to proceed from proof to production and from production to reuse.

Day-60 exit criteria

  • One bounded proof has accountable business, architecture, platform, delivery, governance, and run owners.
  • The six architecture boundaries are explicit enough to guide implementation and review.
  • The team has an agreed evidence plan, pre-mortem, decision log, adoption hypothesis, and stop conditions.
  • The selected proof tests a real enterprise pattern rather than merely showcasing a tool.

Days 61-90

Run one reference pattern under real load.

The proof becomes useful architecture evidence only when the technical design, governance, operating model, economics, and adoption path are exercised together.

Four synchronized workstreams

Solution and integration

  • Translate the contract into components, interfaces, data flows, prompts, retrieval, model routing, orchestration, and failure paths.
  • Prefer reversible choices and explicit seams where platform or model substitution is plausible.
  • Document how the pattern fits existing cloud, data, integration, and automation services rather than creating a parallel estate.

Trust and control

  • Build evaluation around the workflow consequence, not only model accuracy.
  • Test access, lineage, privacy, security, retention, audit evidence, human authority, abstention, and escalation.
  • Use red-team and failure-injection scenarios proportionate to the use case.

Run and economics

  • Instrument traces, logs, metrics, latency, cost, capacity, incidents, recovery, and support demand.
  • Define SLOs, alert ownership, rollback, release controls, and lifecycle accountability.
  • Track unit economics beside performance so hidden operating burden cannot masquerade as value.

Adoption and workflow change

  • Test the pattern with the people who make or act on the decision.
  • Capture exception behavior, trust gaps, training needs, workarounds, and retained use.
  • Update standard work, role expectations, support paths, and feedback loops as part of the architecture—not after it.

Evidence and review cadence

CadenceReview focusDecision
Twice-weekly delivery reviewInterfaces, blockers, tests, controls, and emerging failure modesResolve, escalate, or revise the implementation path
Weekly architecture evidence reviewOutcome, authority, trust, operability, economics, adoption, and reuseProceed, narrow, hold, or stop
Biweekly sponsor readoutBusiness consequence, risk posture, decisions required, and learningProtect focus and remove organizational constraints

Day-90 evidence package

Architecture record
Current-state and target-state diagrams, decision log, interfaces, control design, ownership, and exceptions.
Evaluation record
Test corpus, expected behavior, failure classes, human-review findings, and unresolved risks.
Operating record
SLOs, telemetry, incidents, recovery, capacity, cost, support, and lifecycle responsibilities.
Adoption record
User behavior, workflow impact, training, workarounds, retained use, and feedback-to-change evidence.

Days 91-120

Turn the proof into an enterprise learning system.

The final phase is not a rollout announcement. It is the conversion of evidence into reusable capability, explicit decision rights, a governed exception path, and a visible backlog of architecture-product improvements.

Assets that should survive the proof

Reference architecture
Approved components, seams, interfaces, data authority, control points, deployment options, and legitimate variation.
Delivery kit
Templates, IaC guidance, evaluation harnesses, security and privacy requirements, integration contracts, and example decision records.
Run kit
Telemetry, SLOs, alerts, incident and rollback playbooks, cost model, capacity guidance, ownership, and retirement criteria.
Adoption kit
Workflow changes, role guidance, training, review procedures, support path, feedback loop, and measures of retained use.

Decision-rights model to validate

ActorPrimary decision rightEvidence owed to others
Enterprise architecturePrinciples, reusable patterns, exceptions, lifecycle, and enterprise coherenceClear rationale, usable guidance, and visible tradeoffs
Platform and data ownersPaved-road capabilities, service boundaries, reliability, security, and economicsSLOs, constraints, cost, roadmap, and support commitments
Product and delivery squadsWorkflow design, implementation choices inside the contract, and local evidenceOutcome, tests, integration behavior, incidents, adoption, and exceptions

Balanced architecture scorecard

Day-120 leadership readout

  • Disposition of the bounded proof: promote, revise, hold, retire, or exempt—with evidence and unresolved risks.
  • Reference assets ready for use, owners assigned, and the exception path documented.
  • Architecture-product backlog ranked by recurring squad friction, enterprise risk, and reuse potential.
  • Next-quarter priorities tied to measurable outcome, reliability, trust, economics, adoption, and learning evidence.

Discovery boundary: specific internal platforms, standards, organization design, baselines, and target values must be validated with Thermo Fisher leaders. This plan defines the questions, mechanisms, and evidence—not presumed knowledge of undisclosed internal conditions.
